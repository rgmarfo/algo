BACKGROUND

Bracketing methods in numerical analysis serve as fundamental tools for locating roots of continuous functions within specified intervals. They provide a robust framework for root finding, particularly in scenarios where the behavior of the function is not fully understood or lacks derivative information. At their core, bracketing methods operate on the principle of enclosing a root within an interval, known as a bracket. This bracket is defined by two points where the function values have opposite signs, indicating a root exists somewhere within the interval. The iterative process of bracketing involves progressively refining this interval until the root is pinpointed with the desired level of accuracy. The elegance of bracketing methods lies in their simplicity and universality. They do not require intricate knowledge of the function or its derivatives, relying solely on function evaluations. This makes them versatile tools applicable across a wide range of disciplines and problem domains. The iterative nature of bracketing methods ensures convergence to a root within the bracketed interval. Through systematic interval reduction, the methods guarantee that the root will be found within a finite number of iterations, making them reliable and efficient in practice.
