BACKGROUND

TRAPEZOIDAL RULE

The Trapezoidal Rule is a numerical integration technique used to approximate the definite integral of a function. It works by dividing the area under the curve into trapezoids rather than rectangles, providing a better approximation than the rectangular (or midpoint) rule. The method is derived from the concept of averaging the left-hand and right-hand Riemann sums. Mathematically, for a function \(f(x)\) over the interval \([a,b]\) the integral is approximated as: \[ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx \approx \frac{h}{2} \left[ f(a) + 2 \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} f(x_i) + f(b) \right] \] where \[ h = \frac{b - a}{n} \quad \text{and} \quad x_i = a + ih. \] The Trapezoidal Rule is especially useful for functions that are approximately linear over small intervals and is more accurate with finer partitions
