BACKGROUND

SIMPSON'S RULE

Simpson's Rule is a numerical method used to approximate the definite integral of a function. It is named after the mathematician Thomas Simpson. The rule works by approximating the integrand with a quadratic polynomial. Simpson's Rule provides a better approximation compared to the trapezoidal rule by using parabolic segments instead of linear ones. It applies to functions that are reasonably smooth over the interval of integration. The interval is divided into an even number of subintervals, and the function is evaluated at equally spaced points. The formula combines the values of the function at these points with specific weights. Simpson's Rule is particularly effective when the integrand is well-approximated by parabolas, providing an exact result for polynomials of degree three or lower. This method is widely used in engineering and science for its accuracy and simplicity in implementation.
