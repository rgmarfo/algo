BACKGROUND

GAUSSIAN ELIMINATION METHOD

Gaussian elimination is a fundamental algorithm in linear algebra used to solve systems of linear equations, find matrix inverses, and determine matrix rank. Named after Carl Friedrich Gauss, this method systematically transforms a matrix into an upper triangular form, from which solutions to the equations can be easily obtained. The process involves three main steps: forward elimination, where the matrix is reduced to upper triangular form; back substitution, where the solutions are found by solving the equations from the last row upwards; and pivoting, where rows are swapped to improve numerical stability. Gaussian elimination is not only efficient for small to moderately sized systems but also forms the basis for more complex numerical methods used in computational mathematics. The method is widely applicable in engineering, physics, computer science, and economics, where systems of linear equations frequently arise. Despite its effectiveness, Gaussian elimination can be computationally expensive for very large systems, making alternative methods like LU decomposition or iterative approaches more suitable in such cases. Nevertheless, it remains a cornerstone of linear algebra, integral to both theoretical studies and practical applications.
