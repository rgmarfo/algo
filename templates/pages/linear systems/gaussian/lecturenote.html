Gaussian Elimination Method

Gaussian elimination is a systematic method used to solve systems of linear equations. Named after the German mathematician Carl Friedrich Gauss, this method simplifies the system into an upper triangular matrix, making it easier to solve using back-substitution. It's a fundamental technique in linear algebra and is widely used in various scientific and engineering disciplines.


Objective

The primary goal of Gaussian elimination is to solve a system of linear equations of the form:

\( A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \)

  • A is an \( n \times n \) matrix of coefficients.
  • \(\mathbf{x}\) is a column vector of unknowns.
  • \(\mathbf{b}\) is a column vector of constants.

Steps of Gaussian Elimination

1. Augmented Matrix Formation

Combine the coefficient matrix \( A \) and the constant vector \( \mathbf{b} \) to form an augmented matrix \( [A|\mathbf{b}] \).


Example:

Consider the following system of equations:

\( 2x_1 + 3x_2 - x_3 = 5 \)
\( 4x_1 + 4x_2 - 3x_3 = 3 \)
\( -2x_1 + 3x_2 + 2x_3 = -1 \)

The augmented matrix is:

\[ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 3 & -1 & 5 \\ 4 & 4 & -3 & 3 \\ -2 & 3 & 2 & -1 \end{array} \right] \]

2. Forward Elimination

The goal is to convert the matrix into an upper triangular form where all elements below the main diagonal are zeros.


  • Step 1: Make the first column below the pivot element (first row, first column) zero by performing row operations.
  • Step 2: Move to the next row and repeat the process for each pivot element until the matrix is in upper triangular form.

Example:

The first pivot element is 2 in the first row. Subtract 2 times the first row from the second row:

\( R_2 \rightarrow R_2 - 2R_1 \)

Resulting matrix:

\[ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 3 & -1 & 5 \\ 0 & -2 & -1 & -7 \\ -2 & 3 & 2 & -1 \end{array} \right] \]

Add the first row to the third row:

\( R_3 \rightarrow R_3 + R_1 \)

Resulting matrix:

\[ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 3 & -1 & 5 \\ 0 & -2 & -1 & -7 \\ 0 & 6 & 1 & 4 \end{array} \right] \]

For the next pivot element, make the elements below it zero. Multiply the second row by -3 and add to the third row:

\( R_3 \rightarrow R_3 + 3R_2 \)

Resulting matrix:

\[ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 3 & -1 & 5 \\ 0 & -2 & -1 & -7 \\ 0 & 0 & -2 & -17 \end{array} \right] \]

3. Back Substitution

Once the matrix is in upper triangular form, solve for the unknowns starting from the last equation and moving upward.


Example:

  • From the third row: \( -2x_3 = -17 \implies x_3 = \frac{17}{2} \)
  • Substitute \( x_3 \) into the second row: \( -2x_2 - \frac{17}{2} = -7 \implies x_2 = \frac{3}{2} \)
  • Substitute \( x_2 \) and \( x_3 \) into the first row: \( 2x_1 + 3\left(\frac{3}{2}\right) - \frac{17}{2} = 5 \implies x_1 = 2 \)

Therefore, the solution is:

\[ \mathbf{x} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2 \\ \frac{3}{2} \\ \frac{17}{2} \end{array} \right] \]


Row Operations in Gaussian Elimination

Three types of row operations are allowed during the process:

  • Row Interchange: Swapping two rows.
  • Row Scaling: Multiplying a row by a non-zero scalar.
  • Row Addition: Adding or subtracting the multiple of one row to another.

These operations do not change the solution of the system but help in simplifying the matrix.


Special Cases

  • No Solution (Inconsistent System): If a row is reduced to a form where all coefficients are zero, but the constant term is non-zero (e.g., \( [0 \, 0 \, 0 | c] \), where \( c \neq 0 \)), the system has no solution.
  • Infinitely Many Solutions (Dependent System): If the system has fewer non-zero rows than variables after the elimination process, it has infinitely many solutions. This typically occurs when one or more rows reduce to zeros.

Computational Complexity

The Gaussian elimination method has a computational complexity of \( O(n^3) \) for an \( n \times n \) matrix. This makes it efficient for small to moderately large systems but can become computationally expensive for very large systems.


Applications

  • Solving Linear Systems: Gaussian elimination is widely used in solving linear systems in various fields, including engineering, physics, and economics.
  • Matrix Inversion: The method can also be used to find the inverse of a matrix.
  • Determinant Calculation: Gaussian elimination can simplify the calculation of the determinant of a matrix by transforming it into an upper triangular form.

Advantages

  • Simplicity: The method is straightforward and easy to implement.
  • General Applicability: It can be applied to any system of linear equations, whether underdetermined, overdetermined, or square.

Disadvantages

  • Numerical Stability: The method can be numerically unstable if partial pivoting is not used, especially for systems with very large or very small coefficients.
  • Inefficiency for Large Systems: For extremely large systems, Gaussian elimination can be computationally intensive.

Conclusion

Gaussian elimination is a fundamental method in linear algebra for solving systems of linear equations. Its straightforward approach and wide applicability make it a crucial tool in various scientific and engineering disciplines. Understanding the method and its limitations is essential for effectively applying it to solve real-world problems.
